# The Knowledge Management Handbook

## Summary

Rivera argues that knowledge systems require deliberate, active curation to produce compounding value. Simply capturing information in a structured system is insufficient — without regular pruning, updating, and re-evaluation, knowledge bases become graveyards of stale claims. The book emphasizes that the maintenance cost of a knowledge system grows with its size, and that compounding only occurs when a human operator actively tends the garden.

## Key Themes

- **Active curation over passive accumulation**: Knowledge does not compound on its own. Every note requires periodic review to remain relevant.
- **Maintenance cost scaling**: As a knowledge base grows, the effort required to keep it current grows faster than linearly.
- **Pruning as a core practice**: Removing outdated or contradicted notes is as important as adding new ones.
